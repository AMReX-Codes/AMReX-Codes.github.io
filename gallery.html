Dimethyl Ether Jet

Detailed adaptive simulation of a burning Dimethyl Ether jet with RNS


CLICK to enlarge
Dimethyl Ether (DME) is a promising alternative fuel in the industry, especially for diesel engines and gas turbines, because it can be produced from biomass and has interesting properties in terms of low emission of pollutants. Consequently, there is a need to understand how this fuel behave in the context of turbulent combustion in realistic engine operation conditions. Experimental observations are unable to provide enough details about all the chemical reactions and their interactions with the turbulent flow. Computational Fluid Dynamics of reacting flows is a good candidate to have access to such data, but detailed numerical simulations come at a high computational cost and must rely on accurate numerical methods.

Most CFD codes are not designed to tackle such simulations: some are based on low-order methods that provide inaccurate results; some others implement high-order numerical methods but are unable to deal with the highly stiff chemistry of fuels like DME in the context of turbulent flows, leading in practice to very slow and unpractical simulations time. This has motivated the CCSE team to develop the RNS software. The code solves the compressible reactive Navier-Stokes equations with detailed models for the chemistry. A novel time integration strategy has been developed and is based on the Multi-Level Spectral Deferred Correction method, but extended to an Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR) framework and employs an Implicit/Explicit (IMEX) treatment suitable for combustion applications. Coupled with finite-volume conservative WENO schemes, the RNS code provide an overall fourth-order accuracy in both time and space for all variables, while keeping the computational burden low.

The picture on the left presents an instantaneous snapshot of the temperature field of the Dimethyl Ether flame computed with the RNS code. The simulation employs three levels of mesh refinement, which are depicted with the grey and black boxes.

AMReX is developed at LBNL , NREL , and ANL as part of the Block-Structured AMR Co-Design Center in DOE's Exascale Computing Project. AMReX is under continual development—new changes are first pushed to the development branch and then merged into master at the beginning of each month.
